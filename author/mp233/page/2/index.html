Accused of Spying for China, Until She Wasn’t

May 18, 2015 Mark Tseng Putterman 1 Comment

A recent New York Times feature highlighting the story of Sherry Chen, a Chinese American hydrologist accused of economic espionage, sheds light on the impacts that a Yellow Perilist gaze can have on an individual’s rights and treatment under the law.

Chen, who was born in China but is now a naturalized US citizen, was falsely accused of economic espionage on behalf of the Chinese government based on an innocuous email exchange with an old classmate who happened to be vice minister of China’s Ministry of Water Resources.

Under President Obama, the US has launched the Economic Espionage Act to combat fears that Chinese hackers may be accessing confidential economic information from the United States. As a consequence, the Justice Department has been more aggressively pursuing prosecutions.

From the article:

” ‘They came across a person of Chinese descent and a little bit of evidence that they may have been trying to benefit the Chinese government, but it’s clear there was a little bit of Red Scare and racism involved,’ said Peter J. Toren, a former federal prosecutor who specialized in computer crimes and industrial espionage.”

Read Ms. Chen’s story in full.

Yellow Peril and The Vietnam War

August 3, 2014 Mark Tseng Putterman Leave a comment

50 years after the Gulf of Tonkin resolution created the legal justification for American military involvement in Vietnam, John Kuo Wei Tchen reflects on how “Orientalphobia,” made legible in part through films like Manchurian Candidate, created a climate of fear and paranoia in which misinformation and militarism could thrive.

“In short, the larger racial culture predisposed voters to believe a yellow-peril war script. And the same culture had true believers imagining that the virile Marines would quickly prevail over short, heathen “gooks.” In hindsight, we know none of this was true at that time. We should also know that this paranoid style has not served our nation well in the half century since (WMDs, anyone?). Putting the Gulf of Tonkin “incident” into this larger historical arc helps us understand why, when stereotypes pervade the public imagination, a democratic society can be vulnerable to systemic misinformation.”

Read Tchen’s commentary in full via The Boston Globe.

“The White Man’s Burden (Apologies to Rudyard Kipling),” cartoon, 1899

Image July 29, 2014 Mark Tseng Putterman Leave a comment

 

At the turn of the 20th century, as the United States flexed its imperial muscle in Hawai’i, Puerto Rico, and the Philippines, popular opinion increasingly linked imperial aspirations with a moral obligation to spread Western civilization and values to uncivilized peoples.

This Victor Gillam political cartoon from Judge, a New York-based satirical weekly, caricatures what the “civilizing mission” of Western imperialism promised. Following Great Britain’s John Bull, Uncle Sam is valiantly rescuing oppressed and barbarized peoples clambering up the rocks of ignorance and superstition toward “Civilization,” defined by “education” and “liberty.” However, this vision also evoked criticism and laid the foundations of fear. Was benevolent assimilation possible? What about the “troubles which may follow an imperial policy”?

Victor Gillam, “The White Man’s Burden (Apologies to Rudyard Kipling),” Judge, vol. 36, no. 911 (April 1, 1899). The Ohio State University Billy Ireland Cartoon Library & Museum.

“America the 97 Lb. Weakling?” Fortune cover, 2005

Image July 29, 2014 Mark Tseng Putterman Leave a comment

Here, a muscle-bound beach bully, the People’s Republic of China, is shown intimidating a scrawny Uncle Sam. Robert Sikoryak was commissioned by Fortune to illustrate the cover story “Can Americans compete?” He chose to do a parody of the classic Charles Atlas comic book advertisement. It’s a quirky visual that illustrates the contemporary surfacing of Yellow Peril fears via the perceived economic and political (and at some level, racial) threat of present-day China.

The Sheik, film still, 1921

Image July 29, 2014 Mark Tseng Putterman Leave a comment

Contemporaneous with film adaptations of Sax Rohmer’s Fu Manchu, early Hollywood films similarly depicted the Arab East as a culturally and racially foreign threat to normative America.

Writes Dr. Jack G. Shaheen:

“The Sheik (1921) and The Son of the Sheik (1929) helped solidify several enduring myths: Arab men dominate women, Arab women are subservient harem-dwellers, and the only good Arab is the Western hero (e.g. Rudolph Valentino in The Sheik) clad in Arab garb.”

-Jack Shaheen, A is for Arab: Archiving Stereotypes in U.S. Popular Culture. 

From the Jack G. Shaheen Collection on Arabs in U.S. Film and Television, TAM.535; Tamiment Library and Robert F. Wagner Labor Archives, New York University Libraries.

“The Chinese Must Go,” cap pistol, 1879.

July 28, 2014 Mark Tseng Putterman Leave a comment

The slogan, popularized by infamous California nativist and labor leader Denis Kearney (1847-1907), was a central thematic of the anti-Chinese propaganda that helped fuel the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882.

As with this cap pistol, the slogan was often accompanied by iconography depicting a Chinese man with a queue, being symbolically (and literally) chased or beaten from the United States.

From the Yoshio Kishi and Irene Yah-Ling Sun Collection; Fales Library and Special Collections, New York University Libraries.

The Insidious Dr. Fu Manchu, 1913

Image July 28, 2014 Mark Tseng Putterman Leave a comment

“Imagine a person, tall, lean and feline, high shouldered, with a brow like Shakespeare and a face like Satan, a close-shaven skull, and long, magnetic eyes of the true cat-green. Invest him with all the cruel cunning of an entire Eastern race, accumulated in one giant intellect, with all the resources of science past and present, with all the resources, if you will, of a wealthy government…. Imagine that awful being, and you have a mental picture of Dr. Fu Manchu, the yellow peril incarnate in one man.”

-Sax Rohmer (Arthur Henry Sarsfield Ward), The Insidious Dr. Fu Manchu, 1913.

This was the first of 17 novels selling in the millions. The series’ popularity spawned spin offs in pulps, radio, comics, film, and television. Rohmer based Fu Manchu, the aforementioned “yellow peril incarnate,” on the popular lore of a cunning Chinese gangster in London’s Limehouse district. A Chinese mastermind bent on world domination was a very marketable villain, and Fu Manchu would introduce generations of English-speaking peoples to the imagined threats posed by Asians.

From the Yoshio Kishi and Irene Yah-Ling Sun Collection; Fales Library and Special Collections, New York University Libraries.
