Accused of Spying for China, Until She Wasn’t

May 18, 2015 Mark Tseng Putterman 1 Comment

A recent New York Times feature highlighting the story of Sherry Chen, a Chinese American hydrologist accused of economic espionage, sheds light on the impacts that a Yellow Perilist gaze can have on an individual’s rights and treatment under the law.

Chen, who was born in China but is now a naturalized US citizen, was falsely accused of economic espionage on behalf of the Chinese government based on an innocuous email exchange with an old classmate who happened to be vice minister of China’s Ministry of Water Resources.

Under President Obama, the US has launched the Economic Espionage Act to combat fears that Chinese hackers may be accessing confidential economic information from the United States. As a consequence, the Justice Department has been more aggressively pursuing prosecutions.

From the article:

” ‘They came across a person of Chinese descent and a little bit of evidence that they may have been trying to benefit the Chinese government, but it’s clear there was a little bit of Red Scare and racism involved,’ said Peter J. Toren, a former federal prosecutor who specialized in computer crimes and industrial espionage.”
