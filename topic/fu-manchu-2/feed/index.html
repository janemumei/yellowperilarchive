“Imagine a person, tall, lean and feline, high shouldered, with a brow like Shakespeare and a face like Satan, a close-shaven skull, and long, magnetic eyes of the true cat-green. Invest him with all the cruel cunning of an entire Eastern race, accumulated in one giant intellect, with all the resources of science past and present, with all the resources, if you will, of a wealthy government…. Imagine that awful being, and you have a mental picture of Dr. Fu Manchu, the yellow peril incarnate in one man.”

-Sax Rohmer (Arthur Henry Sarsfield Ward), The Insidious Dr. Fu Manchu, 1913.

This was the first of 17 novels selling in the millions. The series’ popularity spawned spin offs in pulps, radio, comics, film, and television. Rohmer based Fu Manchu, the aforementioned “yellow peril incarnate,” on the popular lore of a cunning Chinese gangster in London’s Limehouse district. A Chinese mastermind bent on world domination was a very marketable villain, and Fu Manchu would introduce generations of English-speaking peoples to the imagined threats posed by Asians.

From the Yoshio Kishi and Irene Yah-Ling Sun Collection; Fales Library and Special Collections, New York University Libraries.
